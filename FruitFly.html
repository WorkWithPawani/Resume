Fruit Fly Investigation

To understand sex linked inheritance using ‘eye-color’, a viable phenotype in Drosophila melanogaster

In 1910, Thomas Hunt Morgan performed an experiment at the Columbia university, in New York city using Drosophila melanogaster that help identifying the role chromosomes play in hereditary. After observing thousands of fruit fly offsprings with red eyes, he obtained one that had white eyes. Morgan began breeding the white eyed mutant fly and found that genetic factor controlling eye color in the flies was on the same chromosomes that determined the gender of the fly. This observation indicated that eye color and sex were both tied to chromosomes and helped Morgan and his colleagues established that the chromosomes carry the genes that allows the offsprings to inherit traits from their parents.

In 1910, Morgan published details of his research in an article titled “sex linked inheritance in Drosophila”. First Morgan took the white mutant and bred it with pure red eyed female flies. All the females that resulted from the breeding had red eyes. Morgan then took the red eyed females and then mated them with the original white eyed mutant male to determine whether the inheritance of the eye color followed by Mendel’s inheritance patterns. If Mendel’s patterns applied to Morgan’s flies there would be one white eye, to every three red eyed flies , that inheritance pattern was not inherited equally across the males and females. Most of the white eyed flies were males. That result indicated that flies did not follow Mendel’s ration in a traditional sense.

After observing the white eyed inheritance pattern, Morgan hypothesized that a factor, or gene, controlling eye color was in the X chromosome. Female flies have to X-chromosomes and males have 1 X-chromosome. If a trait, like eye color corelate with a specific factor on the X-chromosome, then the trait was called X-linked. Because males only have 1 X-chromosome, they display all X-linked traits. Female on the other hand, often need an X-linked trait to exist on both X-chromosomes to display that trait. Morgan hypothesized that, in his breeding experiment, the first generation of flies contained males only with white eyes because the gene controlling eye color was on the X-chromosome.

To test his hypothesis that the white eye trait was on the X-chromosome, Morgan mated other specific groups of flies together and observed the off springs. Prior to doing so, Morgan predicted what the sex and eye color ratios of the off springs would be if his hypothesis was supported. In one mating, Morgan took a white eyed female and mated it with red eyed male. He predicted and observed that half of flies would be red eyed females and the other half would be white eyed males. This mating showed that the occurrence of white eyed trait is limited to the X-chromosome, as only male off-spring could display the white eyed with a single copy of the mutant gene. Morgan showed that inheritance of a trait could differ between sexes. In the following years. Morgan and a group of scientists of Columbia University established the chromosome theory of inheritance, which described the role that chromosomes play in heredity. Thus, we attempt to reproduce the experiments with fruit flies carried out by T. H. Morgan in our laboratory.
